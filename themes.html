2025 Spring Datathon: Themes and Rules

back

Overview

The 2025 Datathon will challenge participants to analyze real-world environmental and operational data to uncover key trends and insights. This year, the event will be more engaging with an interactive model of downtown El Paso, enhancing hands-on data exploration. To encourage greater participation, five preparatory workshops will be offered, equipping participants with essential skills. Participants will have the flexibility to use their preferred tools and software for data analysis and visualization. Final submissions will include visual presentations explaining findings and methodologies, focusing on the real-world impact of their insights.

Exploring the LAKE Units

At the heart of this project are the four LAKE units — LAKE-1, LAKE-2, LAKE-3, and LAKE-4 — which are individual turbines at the Lake Power Station in El Paso, Texas. Each of these units operates as a standalone power generator, running on natural gas and equipped with advanced emissions control technology.

What Kind of Data Do the LAKE Units Provide?

Emissions Data

These values reflect the amount of pollutants each unit releases per day:

  • SO2TONS – Sulfur dioxide emissions (tons/day)
  • NOXTONS – Nitrogen oxides emissions (tons/day)
  • COTONS – Carbon monoxide emissions (tons/day)
  • NH3TONS – Ammonia emissions (tons/day)

Operational Data

These indicators describe how much energy was generated and how the unit was running:

  • UNITONBT / UNITONBA – Energy output (BTU and average)
  • HEATINBT / HEATINBA – Heat input (BTU and average)
  • HEAT_QA – Heat quality check
  • LOADMWBT / LOADMWBA – Power load (megawatts, both total and average)

Weather Data

To better understand each LAKE unit’s behavior, emissions data is paired with daily weather conditions, including:

  • Temperature – tavg, tmin, tmax
  • Precipitation – prcp, and Snowfall – snow
  • Wind – Direction (wdir) and speed (wspd)
  • Pressure – Atmospheric pressure (pres)

Tasks

Your task is to analyze emissions data from the LAKE power units and build predictive models that reveal how environmental factors influence pollution output.Using real historical records from 2021 to 2023, your goal is to uncover patterns, train models, and generate forecasts for specific days in the past and/or future.

1. Understand the LAKE Data

Begin by exploring the daily emissions and operational data for each of the four LAKE units, alongside corresponding weather data.

  • Investigate how emissions levels vary between units and over time
  • Look for trends across seasons, weather conditions, and operational loads
  • Get familiar with pollutant types like SO₂, NOₓ, and CO, as well as key operational metrics

2. Build a Predictive Model

Use machine learning to create a model that predicts daily emissions output based on environmental and operational variables.

  • Train your model using historical data
  • Forecast emissions for selected days (past, future, or both)
  • Predict values for SO2TONS, NOXTONS, and COTONS
  • Evaluate your model’s accuracy using appropriate metrics

3. Analyze Drivers and Compare Unit Performance

Go beyond predictions to understand what’s driving emissions and how each LAKE unit performs under similar conditions.

  • Use your model’s feature importance to identify which variables (like temperature or wind) have the most impact
  • Compare emissions levels across LAKE-1 through LAKE-4 to spot operational differences
  • Identify which units are more efficient or consistent in their emissions output
  • Provide possible explanations for variation between units

Deliverables

Visualization & Presentation

  • This category evaluates how effectively participants communicate their findings using visuals and storytelling.
  • Judges will assess chart clarity, logical flow, and data interpretation. Strong presentations will have engaging visuals, clear takeaways, and structured narratives.
  • Participants should explain their visualizations and connect them to key insights.

Feature Importance Analysis

  • This measures how well participants identify and explain key factors influencing emissions.
  • Judges will look for a clear ranking of features, supported by analysis methods like feature importance scores or correlation analysis.
  • Strong submissions will justify why certain variables matter and connect insights to real-world applications.

Predictive Model Output

  • This assesses the accuracy and justification of the predictive model.
  • Participants should provide forecasts from past and future time frames, evaluate model performance (R², RMSE, etc.), and compare predictions with actual values.
  • Strong presentations will justify model selection and explain its strengths and limitations.
